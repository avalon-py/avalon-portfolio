QuantSim Methodology

A deep dive into the mathematical models, stochastic processes, and assumptions driving the simulation engine.

01 Portfolio Statistics

The core of the simulation relies on accurately estimating the expected return and variance of the combined portfolio. It is insufficient to simply average the returns of the underlying assets.

Expected Return (Portfolio CAGR)

The portfolio's expected return is the weighted sum of individual asset returns.

$$ E[R_p] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i E[R_i] $$
Example A portfolio of 60% SPY (10% return) and 40% BTC (50% return):
$0.60(0.10) + 0.40(0.50) = 0.06 + 0.20 = 26\%$

Portfolio Volatility

Portfolio volatility is calculated using the variance-covariance matrix to account for diversification benefits (correlation).

$$ \sigma_p = \sqrt{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} w_i w_j \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij} } $$

When assets are not perfectly correlated ($\rho < 1$), the combined volatility is lower than the weighted average. This is the mathematical basis of diversification.

02 Monte Carlo Simulation

Monte Carlo simulation is a computational technique that uses random sampling to solve problems that might be deterministic in principle but chaotic in practice. In finance, it allows us to model the "cone of uncertainty" regarding future asset prices.

Why 10,000 Iterations?

We run 10,000 independent simulations to satisfy the Law of Large Numbers. As the number of trials increases, the distribution of results converges on the true theoretical probabilities. 10,000 is an industry standard that balances computational speed (in the browser) with statistical significance for estimating tail risks (like the 5th percentile).

Simulation Logic

  1. Init: Start with Initial Capital.
  2. Loop: For Year 1 to Year N:
  3. Sample random return from distribution.
  4. Update Portfolio: $V_{new} = V_{old} \times (1 + r)$
  5. Subtract Inflation-Adjusted Withdrawal.
  6. Repeat: Do this 10,000 times.
  7. Analyze: Sort results to find percentiles.

03 Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM)

The underlying stochastic process used to model asset prices is Geometric Brownian Motion. This ensures prices generally drift upwards over time but with random volatility, and crucially, prices cannot become negative.

$$ \frac{dS_t}{S_t} = \mu dt + \sigma dW_t $$
  • $S_t$: Portfolio Value
  • $\mu$: Drift (Expected Return)
  • $\sigma$: Volatility
  • $dW_t$: Wiener Process

Discrete Time Implementation

For simulation steps, we use the log-return formula derived via Itô's Lemma.

$$ S_{t+\Delta t} = S_t \exp\left( (\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\Delta t + \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t} Z_t \right) $$

Deep Dive: The Volatility Drag ($-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2$)

You may notice the term $-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ subtracted from the drift. This is a convexity correction derived from Itô's Lemma.

Mathematically, $E[e^X] = e^{E[X] + \frac{1}{2}Var(X)}$. If we didn't subtract the variance term, the expected future value of the simulation would artificially drift higher than the theoretical CAGR.

Intuitive Explanation: Percentage losses hurt more than percentage gains help. A 50% loss requires a 100% gain to recover. This "Volatility Drag" pulls down the realized compound growth rate compared to the arithmetic average. High volatility portfolios ($\sigma^2$) suffer more drag.

04 Fat Tail Events (Jump Diffusion)

Standard GBM assumes returns follow a Normal Distribution. However, real financial markets exhibit "Fat Tails" (excess kurtosis)—extreme events happen far more often than a Bell Curve predicts.

Real World Context: The 1987 Black Monday crash was a ~20 sigma event under standard models. In reality, 30%+ crashes occur every 10-20 years.

Implementation

We use a simplified Jump Diffusion model. There is a fixed annual probability (e.g., 2%) of a "Jump" event occurring. If triggered, we sample from a crash distribution:

$$ Z_{crash} \sim -|N(3, 0.8)| $$

This generates returns in the range of -30% to -60%, simulating events like 2008 (-37%) or the Dot Com bust.

05 Mean Reversion

Markets tend to cycle. Periods of exceptional returns are often followed by stagnation as valuations normalize. We model this by dynamically adjusting the drift $\mu$ based on the simulation's historical performance using an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck logic.

$$ \mu_{next} = \mu_{base} + \kappa (\mu_{target} - \mu_{rolling\_actual}) $$

Variables

  • $\kappa$: Mean reversion speed (0.15)
  • $\mu_{target}$: Expected Portfolio CAGR
  • $\mu_{rolling}$: Actual CAGR of the current path

Example

Target Return: 10%
Path Return (Last 5 yrs): 18% (Bubble)

Adjustment: $0.15 \times (0.10 - 0.18) = -0.012$
Next Year Expected Drift: 8.8% (Lower than avg)

06 Asset Correlations

To accurately simulate a multi-asset portfolio, we cannot generate random returns for each asset independently. We must ensure that when Stocks fall, Crypto likely falls (High Correlation), but Gold might rise (Low/Negative Correlation).

Cholesky Decomposition

We transform a vector of independent random variables $Z_{uncorr}$ into correlated variables $Z_{corr}$ using the Cholesky matrix $\mathbf{L}$ derived from the correlation matrix $\mathbf{\Sigma}$.

$$ \mathbf{\Sigma} = \mathbf{L}\mathbf{L}^T $$ $$ \mathbf{Z}_{corr} = \mathbf{L} \times \mathbf{Z}_{uncorr} $$

This linear algebra transformation ensures that the simulated paths maintain the statistical relationships observed in historical data.

07 Risk of Ruin

Definition: The probability that the portfolio value hits $0.00 (or becomes insufficient to sustain withdrawals) during the simulation period.

$$ \text{Risk of Ruin} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{1}_{\{Min(Path_i) \le 0\}}}{N} \times 100\% $$

For retirees, this is the most critical metric. A portfolio with high returns but high volatility often has a higher risk of ruin than a boring, lower-return portfolio, due to Sequence of Returns Risk.

08 Inflation Adjustment

Inflation acts as a silent killer of wealth. We model inflation as a deterministic drag on purchasing power (default 3%). However, the simulation applies inflation in two distinct ways:

A

Real vs Nominal Returns

The simulation generally displays Nominal portfolio values. To understand purchasing power, one must discount future values:

$$ V_{real} = \frac{V_{nominal}}{(1 + i)^t} $$
B

Withdrawal Indexing

Crucially, if you set a monthly withdrawal of $5,000, the engine increases this amount annually by the inflation rate. In Year 20, you might be withdrawing $9,000 nominal dollars just to buy the same basket of goods.

10 Limitations

  • Constant Correlations: We assume correlations are static. In reality, during crashes, correlations often converge to 1 (everything falls together).
  • Regime Switching: The market has distinct "Bull" and "Bear" states. We currently model this via randomness rather than explicit Markov Chains.
  • Interest Rates: No dynamic modeling of bond yield changes or Fed policy.
